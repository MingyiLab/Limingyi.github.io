Working Papers

Consumption Stimulus with Digital Coupons

with Ying Chen, Jiaming Mao, and Jingyi Zhou. Accepted at JPubE.

Abstract

We study consumption stimulus using digital coupons, which provide time-limited subsidies contingent on minimum spending. Analyzing a large-scale program in China, we find: (1) the program generates large and heterogeneous short-term effects, driven by demand- and supply-side factors; (2) the largest spending increases occur among consumers for whom coupons should be equivalent to cash, suggesting behavioral mechanisms; and (3) high-response consumers disproportionately patronize large businesses, producing regressive benefit allocation. Targeting the most responsive consumers can double total stimulus effects, while a hybrid design combining targeting with direct small business support improves both efficiency and equity.

The Regulator's Dilemma: Land Use Efficiency in the Renewable Energy Expansion

Abstract

This paper evaluates the land-use efficiency of agrivoltaic systems in China, an industrial policy designed to reconcile renewable energy expansion with agricultural preservation. To rationalize the economic frictions inherent in dual-use mandates, I develop a search-theoretic model characterizing firm behavior under imperfect regulatory screening. To test the theoretical predictions, I construct a novel spatial dataset matching administrative records of utility-scale solar farms with precise satellite-derived boundaries, employing a heterogeneity-robust difference-in-differences design against a rigorous counterfactual group of unbuilt projects. I document a stark structural decoupling effect: agrivoltaic system deployment significantly reduces active agricultural production by 7.3 percent relative to the baseline mean, accompanied by a 6 percentage point absolute reduction in physical cropland share. I empirically disentangle the underlying mechanisms, demonstrating that this land abandonment is driven by the super-additive interaction of exogenous spatial search frictions and regulatory arbitrage by private firms. Counterfactual simulations highlight the regulator's dilemma, confronting policymakers with a binding trade-off: maintaining a lenient regulatory environment to accelerate renewable energy expansion inadvertently subsidizes up to ¥247 million annually in pure regulatory arbitrage rents, allowing up to ¥376 million in agricultural value to evaporate through strategic abandonment. Conversely, attempting to recover these losses via strict enforcement acts as a prohibitive tax on spatial search costs, threatening to price out remote developers and sacrifice vital renewable energy capacity.

Balancing the Power of Coal-Fired Power Plants: Growth versus Sustainability

with Ying Chen and Shihe Fu.

Abstract

In emerging markets, power shortages are a major obstacle to economic progress. Coal-fired power plants remain the primary power source in these regions due to their affordability and stability. However, these plants also contribute to severe air pollution and pose a significant threat to public health. This paper examines the impacts and underlying mechanisms of coal-fired power plants on local labor markets by pairing an instrumental variable approach with a quantitative spatial equilibrium model. Our empirical findings indicate competing forces: these plants enhance local firm productivity, driving population inflows, while simultaneously worsening local amenities via air pollution, leading to population outflows. Notably, at the granular county level, the productivity-driven inflow on average exceeds the amenity-driven outflow. By structuralizing these productivity and amenity channels within the quantitative framework, we conduct counterfactual simulations to quantify general equilibrium responses. These simulations uncover significant spatial heterogeneity and complex welfare redistributions resulting from localized capacity shocks. Ultimately, this study highlights the stark spatial trade-offs emerging economies face as they strive to balance the need for a stable electricity supply with environmental hazards.
